# digital authentication

A survey of how digital services prove who people are — sign-up, sign-in, account security, and recovery — across the consumer internet era: the web, mobile phones, and the physical systems that bootstrap them. Organized by the arcs the industry actually followed. Methods that were recently ended, blocked, or advised against, but had a good run, stay listed with their fates marked — a reader remembering a flow they completed in the last fifteen years should be able to find it here. Emerging methods with real government or industry energy behind them are included even where daily use hasn't arrived yet. Facts researched and verified July 2026; implementations are named so every claim can be re-checked.

## The password era and its scaffolding

Where the consumer internet started, and the baseline everything since has tried to improve on.

- **Passwords** — still the near-universal starting point: a shared secret, stored server-side as a salted slow hash (Argon2id is the current recommendation, with bcrypt everywhere and PBKDF2 persisting in FIPS contexts). Modern guidance finally killed the folklore — NIST's rules say no composition requirements, no periodic forced rotation, length over complexity, and check candidates against breached-password corpora (the Have I Been Pwned Pwned-Passwords API, queried by k-anonymity, is the standard). Password managers do the generating and filling: 1Password, Bitwarden, Dashlane, Google Password Manager, and Apple's standalone Passwords app (2024). The identifier half is usually the email address, with unique handles surviving on social products. The strategic story is managed decline: passwords are still how most accounts start, and the thing most of this document is trying to retire.

- **Security questions** — mother's maiden name, first pet, street you grew up on. A pillar of the 2000s that everyone remembers and standards bodies have formally disowned: NIST has disallowed knowledge-based authentication since 2017, because the answers are private-but-not-secret, reused across sites, and scattered through data breaches and social media. They linger in call-center scripts and long-tail banking flows, but no modern design adds them.

- **Security images** — the anti-phishing mutual-authentication ritual of the 2005-2015 online-banking web: the bank shows *you* your chosen picture and phrase ("SiteKey" at Bank of America was the famous one) so you'd trust the page before typing your password. Millions of people picked a lighthouse or a golden retriever. Studies showed users typed their passwords anyway when the image was missing, and the pattern was quietly retired industry-wide. A good run, now gone.

- **The session itself** — after any sign-in succeeds, an httpOnly cookie or rotating token pair is the credential for everything that follows, which makes it the most-used and most-stolen credential on the internet: infostealer malware lifts session cookies wholesale and skips every factor in this document. The countermeasures are long-lived "remember me" sessions paired with device lists and remote revocation, refresh-token rotation, re-authentication before sensitive actions, and — newest — binding sessions to hardware: Google's Device Bound Session Credentials went generally available in Chrome on Windows in 2026, so a stolen cookie stops working off the machine it was minted on.

## Codes over every channel

The one-time code is the workhorse of the mobile era: a short secret sent somewhere you control, typed back to prove you control it. The channels are where the history is.

- **Emailed and texted codes** — a 4-8 digit code to an inbox or phone number proves control of that address, doubling as signup, sign-in, second factor, and recovery. The security load-bearers are unglamorous: rate limits per address, guess limits per challenge, expiry in minutes. Delivery is a product category of its own — Twilio, Vonage, and Sinch for SMS; SES, SendGrid, and Postmark for email; WhatsApp authentication messages as the cheaper SMS alternative Meta sells in India and Brazil; voice-call readout as the accessibility fallback. The SMS half is in managed retreat (see the currents at the end), and pieces have already been pulled: Twitter took SMS two-factor away from non-paying users in 2023 — a change millions experienced as "the way I secured my account is now behind a paywall."

- **Authenticator apps (TOTP)** — RFC 6238 atop counter-based RFC 4226: a shared secret enrolled once by QR (the `otpauth:` URI), then six digits every thirty seconds, computed offline, costing nothing per use. Google Authenticator (cloud-synced since 2023), Microsoft Authenticator, Authy, and the password managers all generate them inline. TOTP's weakness is that it's phishable in real time — proxy kits like Evilginx relay the code as fast as the victim types it — which is precisely the gap passkeys were designed to close. Still the standard self-serve second factor.

- **One-time codes on dedicated hardware** — the pre-smartphone lineage, mostly retired but widely remembered. RSA SecurID fobs put a rolling six-digit code on a keychain LCD and owned enterprise and banking two-factor for two decades (the 2011 breach of RSA's seed infrastructure is a security-history landmark). European banks mailed printed TAN lists and grid cards — cross off a numbered code per transfer — until PSD2's strong-customer-authentication rules ended them in 2019. Yubico OTP put the code in a USB fob that types a 44-character one-time password as if it were a keyboard — the first 12 characters a public device ID, the rest an AES-encrypted counter block, validated by counter ordering rather than clocks (HOTP's cousin, not TOTP's); the keys survive, but their challenge-response successors (the phishing-resistance section below) are why you'd buy one today.

- **Magic links** — the email contains a signed single-use link and the click is the proof; no code to type. Medium has been passwordless this way since 2015 (fifteen-minute, single-use links); Substack defaults to emailed sign-in links; Notion sends a login code with a companion link. Slack — which popularized the term — has moved to emailed codes, and that's the trend: pure link-only flows are disappearing, partly because corporate mail scanners (Outlook Safe Links, Mimecast, Proofpoint, Barracuda) prefetch URLs and consume single-use tokens before the human ever clicks, forcing implementations to add a confirm-button step or scanner detection. The other wrinkle: the link opens in whatever browser the mail app chooses, often not the browser that asked to sign in — so services need a "continue here?" interstitial or end up signing in the wrong device, and everyone ships a code fallback.

- **Silent network authentication** — the carrier confirms possession of the SIM over the mobile data path; no code, no user action, works with eSIM. Sold as Twilio Verify SNA (built from Twilio's 2022 acquisition of Boku's identity unit), Vonage Silent Authentication, Prove (the major US player), and IDlayr (formerly tru.ID); increasingly consumed through the carriers' own standardized API — GSMA Open Gateway / CAMARA Number Verification, the telco industry's current bid to sell identity signals directly.

- **Flash-call verification** — a missed call arrives and the last digits of the caller ID are the code, typed by the user or auto-read by the app. Sinch is the flagship vendor; the economics — cheaper than SMS — made it big in India, Brazil, and Southeast Asia. Now being squeezed out: a Google Play policy change announced July 15, 2026 removes account verification as a permitted use of the call-log permission, killing the silent auto-read that was its main UX advantage, while India's anti-spam crackdowns and carrier hostility (flash calls bypass SMS revenue) press from the other side. Hundreds of millions of phones completed these; the method is fading.

## Federation: someone vouches for you

Outsource the sign-in to a party the user already trusts.

- **Social login (OAuth 2.0 / OpenID Connect)** — delegate sign-in to an identity provider: Google, Apple, Facebook, GitHub, Discord, Microsoft. The site receives a signed token asserting who the provider thinks you are, often with a verified email attached. The classic costs are as well-known as the benefits: the "NASCAR wall" of provider buttons, account-linking collisions when the same email arrives from two providers, dependence on the provider's continued goodwill for your users' access, and the provider seeing every sign-in. Auth.js, Auth0, Clerk, and Supabase Auth are the common integration layers. The ancestor everyone over thirty-five remembers: original OpenID, where you typed your own identity URL into the login box (Stack Overflow's 2009 login page was the canonical sighting) — a decentralized dream that consolidated into the provider buttons and survives mainly in OIDC's name.

- **Sign in with Apple and FedCM** — the newer federation plumbing. Sign in with Apple is OIDC-ish with a private email relay (`…@privaterelay.appleid.com`); App Store guideline 4.8 requires apps offering social login to also offer an equivalent privacy-protecting option, which Sign in with Apple canonically satisfies. FedCM, the browser-mediated sign-in API, shipped in Chrome in 2022 and became mandatory for Google One Tap in August 2025 — and it outlived its own birth story: Google abandoned third-party-cookie deprecation in April 2025 and shut down most of Privacy Sandbox that October, but kept FedCM. Safari hasn't implemented it and Firefox paused, so it's Chromium-only in practice.

- **National login federations** — a bank consortium or government runs the sign-in, and everyone else accepts it. Sweden's BankID (about 99% of adults 18–65, billions of uses a year), Norway's separate BankID (~97%), Belgium's itsme (80%+), Estonia's e-ID smartcards, Singapore's Singpass (97% of residents, 700+ services), India's MeriPehchaan single sign-on over DigiLocker, Australia's myID (renamed from myGovID in 2024), and the UK's GOV.UK One Login consolidating government sign-in. Alongside these sit the enterprise patterns: SAML and OIDC single sign-on, client certificates, smartcards. Where these federations exist they simply *are* identity online; nothing equivalent exists in the US consumer market.

## The multi-device world

Once everyone had two or three signed-in devices, the devices started vouching for each other — like a one-time code, except the delivery channel is the service's own authenticated session instead of email or SMS. The shapes differ in what travels, in which direction, and over what channel.

- **QR sign-in** — the new device displays a QR; the signed-in phone scans it and confirms, and the new device is in. WhatsApp linked devices, Telegram's Log in by QR (the QR rotates every ~30 seconds), Discord (scan from the mobile app, confirm on the phone), Steam Guard QR, LINE, and WeChat — where scanning is the *only* way into web chat — plus TikTok on TVs. WhatsApp also offers the QR-less reverse: the new device shows an 8-digit code you type into the phone.

- **Device authorization codes** — the TV shows a short code and a URL; you finish sign-in on your phone or computer while the TV polls for the result. Standardized as the OAuth 2.0 Device Authorization Grant, RFC 8628. YouTube's youtube.com/activate is the canonical consumer example; GitHub's `gh auth login` and Microsoft's `az login --use-device-code` are the same grant on the command line. Many streaming "enter this code at our site" flows copy the shape without being literal OAuth.

- **Push approval on a trusted device** — the sign-in attempt lands as a notification on the already-signed-in device. Google prompt is a plain approve-or-deny tap, with a tap-the-matching-number variant for riskier sign-ins. Apple's trusted-device flow runs the full loop: every trusted device shows the attempt with a location map, the user taps Allow, and a six-digit code appears on the trusted device to be typed into the new one — the code flows trusted → new, proving the person at the new device holds the old one.

- **Number-matching push** — the hardened form of push approval, built to defeat approval fatigue: the sign-in screen shows a number that must be entered on the trusted device, so approval can only be completed by someone who can see the request. Microsoft Authenticator shows a two-digit number typed into the phone (enforced for all tenants May 8, 2023, after MFA-bombing attacks — the September 2022 Uber breach, where a contractor was pestered and then talked into approving, is the canonical case). Duo Verified Push types a three-to-six digit code into Duo Mobile; Okta Verify shows three candidate numbers on the phone and the user taps the match.

- **In-app code delivery** — the sign-in code arrives inside the service's own signed-in session instead of over SMS. Telegram is the pure case: log in on a new device and the code appears as a Telegram message on your other sessions, with SMS only as a fallback (and a known lockout trap when the old session is gone). Apple's trusted-device code above is the same idea at the operating-system level.

- **Casting and proximity handoff** — open the app on the same Wi-Fi as the streaming device and you're signed in on the TV. Spotify Connect is the clean example: the hardware advertises itself over mDNS (service type `_spotify-connect._tcp`) exposing a small local HTTP API, and the signed-in app calls its `addUser` endpoint with the username and an encrypted token blob — never a password. Google Cast discovery is also mDNS today; the DIAL protocol that Netflix and YouTube co-developed survives mainly in YouTube's TV auto-pairing. Membership on the same network is the proximity proof. Two of this family's best-remembered members are gone: Chromecast's guest mode — which paired devices with an ultrasonic audio code from the TV speakers — was discontinued in 2023, and Netflix removed phone-to-TV casting for virtually all modern devices in late 2025.

## The phishing-resistance turn

The industry's answer to the fact that anything a human can read, a human can be tricked into retyping.

- **Security keys (U2F → FIDO2)** — U2F (Google and Yubico, 2014) replaced typed codes with challenge-response public-key cryptography: touch the key and it signs a challenge bound to the real site's origin, so a phishing page gets nothing usable. U2F evolved into FIDO2/WebAuthn, and the current hardware — the YubiKey 5 series (firmware 5.7 holds 100 device-bound passkeys), Yubico Security Key and YubiKey Bio, Google Titan (250 passkeys), Feitian, Nitrokey — is the roaming, attestable form of the same machinery behind passkeys.

- **Passkeys (WebAuthn/FIDO2)** — the mainstream push of the mid-2020s: a public-key credential created per site, unlocked locally with Face ID, Touch ID, Windows Hello, or a PIN. Phishing-resistant by construction — the credential is bound to the origin, so there is nothing to type into a fake site — and breach-resistant, since the server stores only a public key. The decisive UX change was syncing: passkeys ride iCloud Keychain, Google Password Manager, and Microsoft accounts across a person's devices, with 1Password, Bitwarden, Dashlane, and Proton Pass as third-party providers via the iOS 17+/Android 14 APIs. The unsolved half is lifecycle: enrollment and recovery still fall back to the weaker channels passkeys were meant to replace — lose the ecosystem and you're back to an emailed code — and that asymmetry, plus platform lock-in anxiety, is the substance of most passkey criticism.

- **Passkey cross-device sign-in** — the laptop shows a QR; the phone holding the passkey scans it and answers for it. Defined in CTAP 2.2 as "hybrid" transport, formerly caBLE. A detail worth knowing: Bluetooth is only the proximity check — the phone emits an encrypted BLE advertisement proving it's physically nearby, and the authentication itself travels over an end-to-end-encrypted internet tunnel. The passkey private key never leaves the phone.

## Money as identity: the fintech rails

Payments infrastructure doubles as identity infrastructure — proving you control an account or card proves quite a lot about you.

- **Bank-login account linking** — prove you own a bank account by signing into it through an aggregator embedded in the signup flow. Plaid's Instant Auth covers roughly 95% of eligible US accounts (99.9% counting its fallbacks); the market is Plaid, MX, Mastercard Open Banking (the former Finicity), Visa's Tink in Europe, and TrueLayer. The plumbing is shifting from credential screen-scraping to bank-issued OAuth tokens. The US legal footing is wobbly — the CFPB's open-banking rule (section 1033, finalized October 2024) is enjoined and being rewritten as of mid-2026 — while Europe runs on PSD2 with PSD3 arriving around 2028.

- **Micro-deposits** — the classic: two deposits of $0.01–$0.99 land in your account and you report the amounts. PayPal invented and patented the method (filed 2001) and still offers it. Modern variants: Plaid's same-day flavor sends a single $0.01 deposit whose statement descriptor embeds a three-letter code (`#ABC YourApp ACCTVERIFY`) that you type back; Stripe's bank variant uses a six-digit code starting `SM`; instant versions ride RTP/FedNow and verify in seconds (Plaid, Modern Treasury). India runs the reverse at national scale — the UPI "reverse penny drop," where you push ₹1 to the verifier and your account details come back confirmed, refunded automatically.

- **Card checks and 3-D Secure** — a $0 authorization (sometimes $1, immediately reversed) validates a card with AVS address matching and CVV checks; standard at every processor. PayPal still runs the descriptor-code variant: a $1.95 charge posts with a four-digit code you read off your statement and enter, then it's refunded. 3-D Secure lets the *issuing bank* interject its own challenge mid-checkout — and its first incarnation is a retired flow tens of millions remember: the "Verified by Visa" / "MasterCard SecureCode" pop-up demanding a static password you'd inevitably forgotten. 3DS 1.0 was decommissioned in October 2022; today's 3-D Secure 2 (branded Visa Secure and Mastercard Identity Check, the EMV 3DS standard) challenges with a bank-app push or OTP instead, mostly stays invisible in the background, and is mandatory in the EEA and UK under PSD2's strong-customer-authentication rules, optional but liability-shifting in the US.

- **Knowledge-based authentication** — the credit-bureau quiz: which of these streets have you lived on, which lender holds your car loan. Still sold in 2026 as LexisNexis InstantID Q&A, Experian Knowledge IQ, and IDology ExpectID IQ, but structurally dying: breaches leaked the answers, NIST bars it for both authentication and identity proofing, and the IRS — long its most famous user — replaced it with ID.me verification starting in 2021. It survives as a step-up fallback and in compliance niches; the IRS still requires KBA to e-sign e-file authorization forms.

- **Payroll and employer login** — identity-adjacent: prove employment and income by signing into your payroll provider through an aggregator (Plaid Income's payroll method, Argyle, Pinwheel, Atomic). The consumer-permissioned alternative to Equifax's The Work Number database.

## Proving the person: documents, places, ages

Everything above authenticates an *account*. This family verifies the *person* — the KYC world, pulled onto the mainstream web lately by age-verification law.

- **Document-plus-selfie verification** — photograph a government ID, then pass a liveness-checked selfie that's matched against the document photo. The asynchronous KYC standard for fintech onboarding, age assurance, and high-stakes recovery. The vendor field: Persona, Veriff, Sumsub, Incode, Jumio, Onfido (an Entrust company since 2024), Socure, AU10TIX, Stripe Identity, ID.me. In US government, ID.me covers the IRS and 31 states, and Login.gov's own facial verification went generally available in October 2024 — a one-to-one match against your own ID only, certified at IAL2.

- **Live video KYC** — a momentary video call where a trained agent checks your face against your government ID in real time, with liveness prompts. India's V-CIP (RBI-permitted since January 2020 and treated on par with in-person KYC; requirements include India-only IP addresses, live geotagging, and recorded, audited sessions); Germany's VideoIdent under BaFin rules (IDnow, WebID, Nect, POSTIDENT Video), now being deliberately squeezed toward eID and automated methods; Spain and Switzerland run equivalents under SEPBLAC and FINMA rules. Deepfakes are this method's existential problem, the same as voice.

- **In-person proofing** — present yourself and your documents at a counter. Germany's POSTIDENT at 8,500+ Deutsche Post branches; USPS in-person proofing for Login.gov at 18,000+ post offices (bring the emailed barcode and your driver's license; the clerk scans and inspects); the UK Post Office's identity services with Yoti at 3,000+ branches. The universal baseline that every remote method in this document is trying to replace.

- **Postal mail codes** — a code arrives at your physical address and you type it into your signed-in account: proof of control of a place, not just an inbox, over the slowest OTP channel there is. Google AdSense still mails a six-digit PIN at the $10 earnings mark; Login.gov offers verify-by-mail; Nextdoor's postcard proves neighborhood residency; Japanese banks complete remote account openings with non-forwardable registered mail. Two famous members have faded: Google Business Profile's postcard is now the fallback (video verification became the default), and PayPal's mailed envelope with a code — well remembered from the 2000s — is gone entirely, with current PayPal flows confirming cards and banks instead.

- **Proof-of-address documents** — upload a utility bill, bank statement, or council-tax letter, typically dated within three months, showing your name at the address. Standard in UK and EU bank and crypto-exchange KYC under money-laundering regulations; the document-upload cousin of the postal code.

- **Facial age estimation** — a camera estimates your age band without identifying you: no document, no name, and by design no durable identity at all. Yoti runs it for Instagram, Facebook, Bluesky, and Minecraft; k-ID for Discord; Persona for Reddit's UK checks. The demand is regulatory: the UK Online Safety Act's age-assurance enforcement began in July 2025, the US Supreme Court upheld Texas-style age-verification laws in June 2025 (FSC v. Paxton), and roughly half of US states now have such laws — which is why age checking, not sign-in, is where face scanning meets the mainstream web first.

## Bodies as credentials

- **Airport and venue biometrics** — your face checked by infrastructure someone else owns. TSA runs two distinct systems worth not conflating: CAT-2 credential-check stations at ~350 airports do a one-to-one match of your live face against the photo on the ID you hand over, while PreCheck Touchless ID — at 65 airports as of spring 2026 — is true gallery matching: no ID presented, your face found among pre-staged passport photos, opt-in through Delta, United, American, Southwest, or Alaska. CLEAR moved its ~7.5 million members from iris-and-fingerprint to face-first EnVe pods across its 59 airports. And the family already has a famous casualty: Amazon One, the palm reader at Whole Foods registers and stadium gates, is discontinued for retail and venues effective June 2026, surviving only in healthcare check-in — a reminder that consumer biometrics can simply fail to earn adoption.

- **Enrolled-population biometrics** — enroll a body once, authenticate a population forever. India's Aadhaar is the giant: roughly 1.4 billion people enrolled with ten fingerprints and both irises, 150 billion cumulative authentications and ~2.5 billion more per month, with Aadhaar eKYC as the standard rail for bank and telecom onboarding. The startup version is World (né Worldcoin — the Sam Altman orb): iris scans minting proof-of-personhood, claiming 18M+ verified humans across 160 countries, a US launch in six cities in May 2025, and 2026 verification partnerships with Tinder, Zoom, and Docusign — against bans or suspensions in Spain, Portugal, Hong Kong, Brazil, Indonesia, the Philippines, and Thailand (Kenya investigated, then relented). Proof-of-personhood — this is a distinct live human, one account each — is a different primitive from identity, and AI agents are making it more interesting, not less.

- **Voice biometrics** — call-center voiceprints: HSBC's VoiceID and Schwab's "at Schwab, my voice is my password" are still running in 2026. But journalists cloned their own voices to beat Lloyds' voice ID and Australia's Centrelink back in early 2023, and the industry's posture is to layer deepfake detection and shift weight toward passkeys and device signals rather than retire voiceprints outright. A weakening signal, not yet a dead one.

- **Trust signals that aren't proofs** — the ambient layer around everything in this document: trusted-device lists ("remember this browser"), new-device sign-in alert emails, risk-based step-up like GitHub's sudo mode (re-authenticate before sensitive actions, with a roughly two-hour window), behavioral biometrics passively scoring typing cadence and mouse movement (BioCatch — used by 30+ of the world's 100 largest banks, analyzing 18 billion sessions a month; LexisNexis BehavioSec; Mastercard NuData), device attestation where the platform vouches that the app and hardware are genuine (Apple's DeviceCheck and App Attest; Google's Play Integrity, which fully replaced the long-lived SafetyNet in January 2025), and bot gates like Cloudflare Turnstile and reCAPTCHA, which prove a visitor is probably human without proving anything durable about which human.

## Recovery: when everything is lost

- **Recovery machinery** — the industry's answers to locked-out-of-everything. Recovery keys: Apple's 28-character code, which when enabled *replaces* standard account recovery — lose the key and the trusted devices and the account is gone permanently (Dashlane uses the same shape; Bitwarden pointedly offers no master-password recovery at all, only a 2FA-bypass code and trusted-contact Emergency Access — unrecoverability as a feature). Recovery contacts went mainstream: Apple allows five (your contact reads you a six-digit code from their device), and Google launched theirs in October 2025 with up to ten (direction reversed — you read a code to your contact, who confirms it). Facebook's Trusted Contacts, the pioneer that let three-to-five friends rescue your account, had its run from 2013 and was discontinued in 2023. The heavyweight backstop is ID-document re-verification with a selfie (ID.me with a video-agent fallback, Persona).

## The emerging wave

Methods with government or industry energy behind them — standards published, pilots running, trade press loud — whether or not they've reached daily user flows yet. Some will fail; familiarity arrives ahead of adoption either way.

- **Wallet-held digital IDs and selective disclosure** — a government-issued credential in your wallet app, presented attribute-by-attribute: the site asks "over 18?" and receives a cryptographically signed yes from the issuing authority without ever seeing your name or birthdate. Europe is the forcing function: the eIDAS 2.0 EU Digital Identity Wallet must be offered by every member state by December 2026, six countries are piloting the age-verification blueprint, and large platforms and regulated sectors must accept the wallet by late 2027. The zero-knowledge-proof angle is real engineering, not brochure-ware — the EU blueprint uses ZK-style selective disclosure, and Google shipped open-source ZKP libraries for its Wallet IDs in 2025 so a site can verify an age threshold without the issuer learning where you used it. In the US the same rails arrive as state mobile driver's licenses (ISO 18013-5): fourteen states plus Puerto Rico in Apple Wallet, a smaller set in Google Wallet, TSA acceptance at 250+ airports, and state apps like myColorado. The web on-ramp shipped in fall 2025: the W3C Digital Credentials API is in stable Chrome 141 and Safari 26, speaking OpenID4VP and ISO 18013-7. Distinct from BankID-style national login: the credential lives with the person and discloses attributes; the issuer isn't running the sign-in.

- **Verifiable credentials beyond government** — the same holder-presents-a-signed-claim architecture (W3C Verifiable Credentials, DIDs) applied to everything else: employment credentials, professional licenses, university degrees, reusable KYC. Microsoft Entra Verified ID is the biggest commercial push; the reusable-identity products (Yoti's digital ID, Sumsub's reusable KYC) circle the same idea. Years of trade-press noise, thin daily-flow adoption so far — the EUDI wallet is likely the thing that finally normalizes the pattern.

- **Crypto wallet sign-in** — possession of a private key as the whole identity. Sign-In with Ethereum (EIP-4361) is the standard: the site issues a nonce, the wallet (MetaMask, WalletConnect, Coinbase Wallet) signs a structured message, and the server verifies that the signature matches the claimed address — smart-contract wallets verify on-chain via EIP-1271. No email, no name, no recovery beyond the seed phrase; cousins exist for Solana and Farcaster. A decade of energy, still niche outside crypto-native products — but it's the purest possession credential on this list, and the self-sovereign end of the same wallet spectrum governments are now entering from the other side.

- **Credentials for AI agents** — the newest noise: when software agents shop and book on a person's behalf, something has to prove the agent is authorized and the human behind it is real. The card networks moved first — Visa's Intelligent Commerce and Mastercard's Agent Pay (both announced 2025) define tokenized, scoped payment credentials for agents — while the identity industry sketches delegated OAuth flows and agent registries, and proof-of-personhood products (World ID above) pitch themselves as the human-side anchor. Almost none of this is in daily flows yet; all of it is loud.

## The currents underneath

Three currents worth knowing while scanning. First, SMS OTP is being regulated out: CISA's December 2024 guidance, issued after the Salt Typhoon telecom compromise, says flatly "do not use SMS as a second factor"; NIST classifies SMS as a "restricted" authenticator; Singapore's banks have already dropped SMS OTP for logins and card payments, with the UAE and Philippines on 2026 phase-out deadlines. Second, passkeys crossed the mainstream line: NIST SP 800-63-4 (July 2025) recognizes synced passkeys at AAL2, roughly five billion are in active use, about half of the top-100 websites support them, and Microsoft made new consumer accounts passkey-first in 2025. Third, deepfakes are hollowing out remote-human verification — voice and live video — while age-verification law and eIDAS push toward wallet-held credentials and selective disclosure. The direction of travel favors the phishing-resistant and cross-device families above.
